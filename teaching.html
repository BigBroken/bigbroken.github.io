Teaching

Instructor of record, Fall 2020 (‘Antarctica: the Coolest Continent’, NATSCI 191-01 and -02): I designed and taught an introductory course to incoming first-years. This course explored a range of different topics all relating to Antarctic science, for example: the past and future of the ice sheet; basic climate science and its applications; art and culture in Antarctica; the geopolitical setting and history of Antarctic exploration; and how all these factors intertwine with issues of diversity and inclusion in Antarctic science today.

For their final projects, I asked students to delve deeper into class material by further exploring a topic relating to Antarctic science through the medium of their choice. This resulted in a variety of creative expressions as students explored their own interests and gained new knowledge and perspectives. With students’ permission, I include some examples below.

  1. “This is a painting of where ice meets the ocean in Antarctica. I especially liked this idea because I did not include any scaling features. This allows for space to imagine how grand this ice wall could be... or how small. Putting the ice in perspective would make it seem like I know more about the intricacies of the ice than I actually do. Its size is completely ambiguous, reflecting the varying expanses and shapes of the continent's features. I wanted to make something that would remind people about how little we know about the space as a whole. I am hoping that this would incite the curiosity of the viewer, which would lead them to make choices for the sake of the ice and scientific discovery. To destroy the ice (by cutting through it with ships to get to oil/trade or indirectly by contributing to global warming in their day-to-day) is to destroy the possibility of gaining knowledge and melting away natural beauty. There are people working very hard to restore natural scapes, but if we can save the land before it is ruined, that can be avoided entirely. Small changes lead to larger ones, so there is always hope for the coming times.” It is acrylic on canvas (7"X11") called: The Tempo of Blue
